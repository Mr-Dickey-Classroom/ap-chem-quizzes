Background & Theory

A calorimeter is a device used to measure the heat transferred during a physical or chemical process. In this lab you will use a simple coffee cup calorimeter — two nested styrofoam cups with a lid — which approximates an isolated system at constant pressure.

q = mcΔT
q = heat (J)  |  m = mass (g)  |  c = specific heat capacity (J/g·°C)  |  ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial (°C)

The specific heat capacity of water is 4.184 J/g·°C. For dilute aqueous solutions, we assume the same value. By the law of conservation of energy:

qsystem = −qsurroundings

An exothermic process releases heat to the surroundings (ΔT > 0, q < 0 for system). An endothermic process absorbs heat from the surroundings (ΔT < 0, q < 0 for surroundings).

🔬 Real vs. Ideal Calorimeter
A real calorimeter absorbs some heat itself — this is the calorimeter constant (Ccal), measured in J/°C. Experiment 1 will help you determine Ccal for your virtual calorimeter. In Experiment 2, you will account for it in your calculations.

Experiment 1: Mixing Hot & Cold Water

CED 6.3, 6.4

Purpose: When hot and cold water are mixed, energy is transferred until thermal equilibrium is reached. By comparing the theoretical final temperature (assuming a perfect calorimeter) with the actual measured temperature, we can determine how much energy the calorimeter itself absorbed — the calorimeter constant, Ccal.

🧠 Key Concept
In a perfect calorimeter: qhot lost = qcold gained. In reality, the calorimeter absorbs some energy too:
qhot + qcold + qcal = 0, so Ccal = −(qhot + qcold) / ΔTcal

Experiment 2: Heat of Solution

CED 6.1, 6.6

Purpose: When an ionic solid dissolves in water, energy is either released or absorbed. You will measure the temperature change when three different salts dissolve in water, calculate qsoln for each, and determine the molar enthalpy of solution (ΔHsoln) in kJ/mol.

🧮 Key Formula
ΔHsoln (J/mol) = −qsoln / n
where qsoln = (mwater + msalt) × c × ΔT  and  n = moles of salt dissolved
(Include the calorimeter correction: qtotal = qsoln + Ccal × ΔT)
