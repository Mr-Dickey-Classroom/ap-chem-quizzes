Reflection of Light

CK-12 Ch 15.1
Background: When light hits a smooth surface, it bounces back at the same angle it came in. The Law of Reflection states that the angle of incidence (θᵢ) always equals the angle of reflection (θᵣ) — both measured from the normal (a line perpendicular to the surface).

Regular reflection occurs on smooth mirrors (parallel rays stay parallel). Diffuse reflection occurs on rough surfaces (rays scatter in all directions).
Law of Reflection:    θᵢ = θᵣ

Refraction of Light

CK-12 Ch 15.2–15.3
Background: When light travels from one medium to another, it changes speed — and if it hits the boundary at an angle, it bends. This bending is called refraction.

The index of refraction (n) tells us how much a material slows light down compared to a vacuum. A higher n means slower light and more bending. Snell's Law lets us calculate exactly how much the ray bends.
Snell's Law:   n₁ sin(θ₁) = n₂ sin(θ₂)   |   Index of Refraction:   n = c / v
Common Indices of Refraction:   Vacuum = 1.000  |  Air ≈ 1.000  |  Water = 1.333  |  Glass = 1.500  |  Diamond = 2.417

Total Internal Reflection

CK-12 Ch 15.3
Background: When light travels from a denser medium to a less dense medium (like water → air), and the angle of incidence is large enough, the light cannot escape — it reflects entirely back into the denser medium. This is called Total Internal Reflection (TIR). The minimum angle at which this occurs is called the critical angle (θc). Fiber optic cables rely entirely on this principle!
