PhET: Gas Properties
Learning Target
Confirm that the total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of each component gas, at constant temperature and volume.
Student Information
Dalton's Law Formula
Ptotal = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...
Sum of all partial pressures = Total pressure
Key Concepts to Remember
  • 🔵 Pressure depends on number of collisions, not particle mass
  • 🔶 Heavy & light particles have the same average KE at equal T
  • ⬜ Gases in a mixture act independently
PART 1
Pre-Lab Setup & Controlled Variables
Setup Instructions — Complete These Steps in Order
1

Open the simulation below. When it loads, you'll see a screen with two options — click the left tile (Ideal) to enter the correct simulation screen. The screen shows a gas container with a pressure gauge on the right side.

2

Add 50 heavy particles first using the Heavy pump arrows (+) in the top-right panel. You must add particles before the "Hold Constant" option becomes available. The particle count appears in the box next to the arrows.

3

Now set Hold Constant → Temperature (T) in the right panel. This locks temperature at 300 K (the default) so pressure changes are caused only by particle count. You'll see "300 K" displayed on the thermometer.

4

Check the "Width" checkbox in the right panel. A ruler will appear — read the width of the container and record it as your volume below. Do not resize the container during the lab.

5

Confirm your starting state: Temperature = 300 K, Hold Constant = Temperature (T), Width checkbox checked. You're now ready to collect data! Record the controlled variables below before continuing.

6

Wait 5–10 seconds after each particle change for the pressure to stabilize before recording. The pressure gauge needle will stop swinging when it's ready. Read to the nearest 0.01 atm.

⚠️
When switching between heavy-only and light-only trials, use the minus (−) arrows to bring that particle type back to zero before adding the new type. For Phase C mixture trials, keep both types in the container at the same time.

PART 3
Post-Lab Analysis Questions
Question 1 — Pattern Recognition
Looking at your data from Phase C, how does the measured total pressure compare to the sum of the individual partial pressures (Phase A + Phase B) for the same particle counts? State a trend or pattern you observe.
💡 Hint: Compare your teal "predicted" values with your measured values. Are they close? What does that tell you?
Question 2 — Mass & Pressure
Compare the pressure of 100 heavy particles to 100 light particles at the same temperature and volume. Does the mass of the gas particles affect pressure? Use your data as evidence to support your answer.
💡 Hint: Think about the Kinetic Molecular Theory — what do heavy and light particles have in common at the same temperature?
Question 3 — KMT Connection
The simulation note says that at the same temperature, heavy and light particles have the same kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²). If the heavy particle is more massive, what must be true about the speed of the light particle? Explain why this matters for pressure.
Question 4 — Dalton's Law in Words
In your own words, write a statement of Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures based on your experimental results (not the textbook definition). Reference at least one specific data point from your table.
Question 5 — Sources of Error
Even in a simulation, you may notice small differences between the predicted sum (Pheavy + Plight) and the measured total pressure. List at least two reasons why these values might not match perfectly.
💡 Think about: reading the pressure gauge, simulation randomness, particle fluctuations, decimal rounding...
