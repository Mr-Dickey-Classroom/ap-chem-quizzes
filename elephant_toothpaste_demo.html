Rate Law Breakdown

Uncatalyzed

2 H₂O₂(aq) → 2 H₂O(l) + O₂(g)
Rate = k[H₂O₂]
k = 7.5×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹ at 22.2°C
Eₐ = 75 kJ/mol

KI-Catalyzed (Two-Step Mechanism)

Step 1 (slow/RDS): H₂O₂ + I⁻ → HOI + OH⁻
Step 2 (fast):    H₂O₂ + HOI → H₂O + O₂ + I⁻
─────────────────────────────────
Net: 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂
I⁻ consumed in Step 1, regenerated in Step 2 → catalyst!
Rate = k₂[H₂O₂][I⁻] → k' = k₂[I⁻] = 0.202 s⁻¹
Eₐ = 56 kJ/mol
💡 This is a perfect AP 5.11 example: I⁻ is consumed in the RDS (Step 1) and regenerated in Step 2 — so net [I⁻] is unchanged. HOI is the reaction intermediate.

Pequa "Super Cess-Flo" — Unknown Concentration

Your Pequa product is labeled 10–20% H₂O₂. Here's how to bracket the unknown concentration using foam behavior:

If the foam…Estimated [H₂O₂]
Barely reaches 15 cm in a 500 mL cylinder~10%
Fills a 500 mL cylinder about halfway~15%
Overflows a 500 mL cylinder~20%
💡 Recommendation: Assume 15% as your starting estimate. Run a 25 mL test trial in a 250 mL graduated cylinder before the full demo to calibrate.
Stoichiometry Calculation
2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂
→ 1 mol H₂O₂ produces 0.5 mol O₂

mol H₂O₂ = [H₂O₂] × V(L)
mol O₂ = mol H₂O₂ × 0.5
V(O₂) = nRT/P = n × 0.08206 × 295.4 K

Example: 20%, 50 mL
mol H₂O₂ = 6.35 M × 0.050 L = 0.318 mol
mol O₂ = 0.318 × 0.5 = 0.159 mol
V = 0.159 × 0.08206 × 295.4 = 3.85 L

Procedure
  1. Set graduated cylinder in the center of a large tray. Add food coloring to the inside of the cylinder for visual effect.
  2. Measure 50 mL of Pequa H₂O₂ into the cylinder. Add 1–2 squirts of dish soap and swirl gently — do not create bubbles yet.
  3. In a separate small cup, dissolve 1–2 g KI in 10 mL warm water to make the catalyst solution (~0.5 M).
  4. Hold the KI solution above the cylinder. Ask students: "What do you predict will happen? Why?"
  5. Pour the KI solution directly into the cylinder in one motion. Step back and observe.
  6. While foam forms, ask: "What gas is being produced? How do you know? What is the role of KI?"
  7. Touch the outside of the cylinder — students should feel it is warm. Ask: "What does that tell us about the reaction?"
Safety
🚨
H₂O₂ at 10–20% is a strong oxidizer.
• Wear nitrile gloves and safety goggles at all times
• Avoid skin/eye contact — flush immediately with water if contact occurs
• Do not use concentrations above 30% without fume hood
• Foam is hot — do not touch immediately after reaction
• Dispose of foam/liquid down the drain with excess water
⚠️ KI is non-toxic but the iodine produced transiently (HOI intermediate) gives a brief brown color before being consumed. This is the intermediate in action — point it out to students!
AP Discussion Questions (During/After Demo)
  1. The bottle of Pequa has been sitting in the supply closet for months with no visible change. What does this tell you about the uncatalyzed rate? (t½ ≈ 2.9 years at room temp)
  2. You added KI and the reaction was complete in ~10 seconds. Did the KI get used up? How do you know? What does this confirm about its role?
  3. Write the two elementary steps and identify: the catalyst, the intermediate, and the rate-determining step.
  4. The cylinder felt warm after the reaction. Draw a qualitative energy profile showing both the uncatalyzed and catalyzed pathways. Label Eₐ and ΔH for each.
  5. If you repeated this with 20% H₂O₂ instead of 10%, predict how the foam height and rate would change. Would t½ change?
  6. This reaction is first-order in H₂O₂. If you doubled [H₂O₂] but kept [KI] the same, by what factor would the initial rate change?

Integrated Rate Law Linearization — H₂O₂ Decomposition

The integrated rate law lets us determine reaction order from a graph. We plot the same concentration-vs-time data three different ways. Only the transformation that matches the true order produces a straight line. The slope of that line gives us k directly.

0th order: [A]t = [A]0 − kt
Plot [A] vs t → linear, slope = −k
1st order: ln[A]t = ln[A]0 − kt
Plot ln[A] vs t → linear, slope = −k
2nd order: 1/[A]t = 1/[A]0 + kt
Plot 1/[A] vs t → linear, slope = +k

Step 1 — Temperature Conversion
T(°F) = 72°F
T(°C) = (72 − 32) × 5/9 = 40 × 5/9 = 22.2°C
T(K) = 22.2 + 273.15 = 295.4 K
Step 2 — Molar Concentrations from % w/w
density ≈ 1.0 + 0.004 × (% H₂O₂) [g/mL, approx]
g H₂O₂ per L = % × ρ × 10
[H₂O₂] = g/L ÷ MW = g/L ÷ 34.015 g/mol

10%: ρ=1.040 → 104.0 g/L ÷ 34.015 = 3.06 M
15%: ρ=1.060 → 159.0 g/L ÷ 34.015 = 4.67 M
20%: ρ=1.080 → 216.0 g/L ÷ 34.015 = 6.35 M
30%: ρ=1.120 → 336.0 g/L ÷ 34.015 = 9.88 M
Step 3 — Arrhenius: k at 22.2°C
ln(k₂/k₁) = (Eₐ/R)(1/T₁ − 1/T₂)
k at 22.2°C = k_ref × exp[−Eₐ/R × (1/T − 1/T_ref)]

Uncatalyzed (Eₐ = 75,000 J/mol):
k_ref = 1.0×10⁻⁸ s⁻¹ at 298.15 K
factor = exp[−(75000/8.314)(1/295.4 − 1/298.15)]
= exp[−9020 × (−3.13×10⁻⁵)]
= exp[−0.284] = 0.752
k_uncat = 1.0×10⁻⁸ × 0.752 = 7.52×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹
t½ = 0.693 / 7.52×10⁻⁹ = 9.2×10⁷ s ≈ 2.9 years

KI-Catalyzed (Eₐ = 56,000 J/mol):
k₂_ref = 0.50 M⁻¹s⁻¹ at 298.15 K
factor = exp[−(56000/8.314)(1/295.4 − 1/298.15)]
= exp[−0.211] = 0.809
k₂ = 0.50 × 0.809 = 0.404 M⁻¹s⁻¹
k' = k₂ × [KI] = 0.404 × 0.5 = 0.202 s⁻¹
t½ = 0.693 / 0.202 = 3.4 s
Step 4 — Initial Rates
Rate = k[H₂O₂] (first-order in H₂O₂)

Uncatalyzed, k = 7.52×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹:
10%: 7.52×10⁻⁹ × 3.06 = 2.30×10⁻⁸ M/s
20%: 7.52×10⁻⁹ × 6.35 = 4.78×10⁻⁸ M/s
30%: 7.52×10⁻⁹ × 9.88 = 7.43×10⁻⁸ M/s

Catalyzed, k' = 0.202 s⁻¹:
10%: 0.202 × 3.06 = 0.618 M/s
20%: 0.202 × 6.35 = 1.284 M/s
30%: 0.202 × 9.88 = 1.997 M/s
Step 5 — O₂ Volume (Stoichiometry)
2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂
1 mol H₂O₂ → 0.5 mol O₂

V = nRT/P, R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K
T = 295.4 K, P = 1.00 atm

20% H₂O₂, 50 mL solution:
n(H₂O₂) = 6.35 × 0.050 = 0.318 mol
n(O₂) = 0.318 × 0.5 = 0.159 mol
V(O₂) = 0.159 × 0.08206 × 295.4
= 3.85 L = 3,850 mL at 22.2°C
Step 6 — Rate Enhancement Factor
k_cat / k_uncat = 0.202 / 7.52×10⁻⁹
= 2.69 × 10⁷

The catalyst makes the reaction ~27 million times faster.

Equivalently:
Uncatalyzed: 90% done in 3,543 days (9.7 yr)
Catalyzed: 90% done in 11.4 seconds

This is the macroscopic signature of lowering Eₐ
from 75 → 56 kJ/mol (ΔEₐ = −19 kJ/mol)
